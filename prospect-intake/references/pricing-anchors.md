# Pricing anchors

Reference numbers for proposing pre-sale pricing, derived from the two signed clients. **Propose,
don't commit** — these seed `proposal/draft-pricing.md`; the operator finalizes on the call.

## The model (both clients)

Two components plus pass-throughs:

1. **One-time onboarding / setup fee, paid up front.** Covers the initial audit, planning, kickoff
   outcomes, and the first wave of structural work (GBP setup, first pages, site-defect fixes).
2. **Value-based monthly retainer that starts only when the client books their first paying
   customer through the work.** The retainer scales with call volume — as their leads grow, the
   retainer grows. "Pay for value, not for hours." No retainer is owed during ramp-up before the
   work produces revenue.
3. **Client pays directly, no markup:** tool subscriptions (SEO tools, page builders, automation),
   Chamber of Commerce / community-signal memberships, sponsorships, paid directory listings.

This aligns incentives — Oliver earns more when the client earns more.

## How to propose for a new prospect

- **Default onboarding fee: $700–$950.** Start at $700; go higher if (a) the prospect mentions a
  competing quote above that, (b) the site needs heavy remediation, or (c) they're clearly a
  larger operation. Use $500 only for a friend/returning-client rate.
- **Retainer: leave as "value-based, begins on first booked job, scales with volume"** and a
  placeholder range rather than a hard number — it's negotiated live against their average ticket
  and target volume. If they share an average job value on the call, the retainer conversation
  anchors there.
- **Always state the pass-through rule** (they pay tooling/community directly, no markup) — it's a
  trust signal and it keeps the retainer clean.
- **Anchor to cost of inaction.** "You're losing ~N jobs/month to [competitor]; the onboarding fee
  is a fraction of one job." Pull the N from the competitor glance + their average ticket.
